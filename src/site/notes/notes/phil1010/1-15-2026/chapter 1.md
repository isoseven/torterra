## what is critical thinking?
- not what you're thinking or reasoning but why you're accepting/refuting an idea.
### vaughn, pp. 2-3
- your beliefs are yours only if you critically examine them for yourself.
- otherwise you are just a collection of the ideas around you
> [!info] 
> Most things in this class will be due to "If you have good reason to do so"
## how to use critical thinking
- Group Project Dynamics
	- (Does this idea address the rubric? What evidence supports this?)
- Deciding Whether to Join a Club
	- (How much time will this take? Does it align with my goals? Are the benefits real?)

## why does it matter?
- post-truth fog
	- "**post-truth**" : the state of the world now. it is becoming acceptable to say something without any backing; lying is widely expected and truth is no longer important :(
	- truth is no longer the standard, so we need to use critical thinking in order to **discern what is true or false**.

## argument
- a group of **statements** in which some of them (the **premises**) are intended to support another of them (the **conclusion**).
	- **statement**: an assertion that something is or is not the case (a boolean)
		- **premise**: a statement that is given as a reason to support another statement (the conclusion)
		- **conclusion**: a statement that the premises are intended to support.
- **statements can be proven. they are either Yes / No**
	- Statements / Not statements
- arguments must have **at least** one premise and one conclusion.

## assertions, explanations, persuasion
- **assertion**: a declaration of beliefs; no evidence or reasons to believe
	- "being able to distinguish between passages that **do and do not contain arguments** is a very basic skill and an extremely important one."
	- a mere declaration of beliefs is **NOT** an argument. that is an assertion.
- **explanation**: asks for a cause (why is it?). **argument**: asks for evidence (how do you know?).
	- arguments require support and evidence, where explanation does not have any.
- **persuasion**: "to cause someone to do something through reasoning or argument"
	- an **argument** can be a form of persuasion, but **not all persuasion** involves an argument.
	- not all persuasion involves good reasons!
