1. by reading laterally, you can quickly do at least 3 things
	1. determine who's behind the info you're seeing
	2. uncover the purpose or motivation behind info
	3. find out how credible the sources are
2. what are the key questions to ask when reading critically
	1. are the claims plausible?
	2. what is the support for the claims?
	3. have reliable fact-checking organizations examined the claims?
3. important tips for using wiki/google
	1. use google scholar for scholarly articles
	2. look at trustworthy domains
	3. search inside websites with "site:"
	4. check the resource list / sources as you read
4. some ways for us to check our biases
	1. go out of your way to look for disconfirming and confirming evidence


## reasonable skepticism and images **
- determine the source
	- where did the image come from? who posted it, and do they have any bias/credibility?
	- if a photo is posted by someone with a history of fake news, it may be a fake image.
	- **look at "pictures in history" on facebook and see if any fake images/news have been posted**
- check for previous uses
	- make sure the image is used in the correct context; images may be reused and edited to prove a point.
	- use a reverse image search to make sure any photos have been reused or edited to fit a claim.
	- **the photo was a doctored image of chris hadfield with easter eggs in a bag originally posted in 2013**
- be wary of the incredible
	- photos that are too amazing to be true are probably not true.
	- fictional characters or myths appearing in images may be edited or fake (bigfoot sightings or ufo sightings)
	- **it is not very plausible for an astronaut to sneak marijuana into space**
- look for incongruities
	- check for signs of editing in images, and make sure details match reality
	- look for ai artifacts (missing fingers, strange lighting)
	- **weird lighting on the bag, low quality photo .. etc**
