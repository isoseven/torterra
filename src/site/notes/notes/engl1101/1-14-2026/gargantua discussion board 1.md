In Rabelais' _Gargantua and Pantagruel_, Gargantua is exposed to many areas of knowledge, picking up many different kinds of literacies as part of the social ideal of chivalry. Although in a completely different era and many drastic differences in the ways of life of medieval people, Gargantua is still taught many of the ideas and literacies that are still prized today.

A literacy that Gargantua was taught was civil literacy, or social skills in today's terminology. Gargantua was deliberately taught "lighter pastimes which use cards, dice or tumblers" (Rabelais 284) as a way to teach a certain manner of chivalry. Although civil literacy may not seem as important as religious piety or skill in combat, it was still taught because it was seen as a crucial skill at the time.

In modern times, civil literacy is still developed, but less as learning and more as a method of enjoyment or reward. Although the literacy itself has not died, as many people are inclined to enjoy themselves, the exclusion of such literacy of lighter subjects have made learning into something of a chore. This idea that socializing equates to fun and learning does not is accidentally implemented by leaving a major part of development out of many learning systems.

Ultimately, Rabelais' *Gargantua and Pantagruel* shows what an ideal learning experience was during the medieval times. Today's systematic approach to learning may cause problems in certain areas of literacy, and the idea of how Gargantua learns should be taken into account when trying to teach efficiently.
