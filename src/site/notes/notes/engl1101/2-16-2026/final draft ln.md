I play Tetris.

In a world full of surreal virtual dimensions and epic adventure games with mind-blowing graphics, I play a game about stacking blocks in a grid. But my Tetris is not the typical black-on-green puzzle game that might come to mind; it's an all-out battle of wits and endurance where strategy and skill reign supreme.

My fateful journey into the world of Tetris started with a video showcasing the skills of a player named “Doremy”. I watched, stunned, as Doremy effortlessly commanded blocks to fly across the screen with a few taps on a keyboard. I spiraled into a Youtube rabbit hole as I envisioned myself pulling off this feat.

Drawn towards the allure of playing like Doremy, I logged onto TETR.IO (the most popular form of modern Tetris at the time), created an account under the username “WYVR”, and relentlessly practiced night after night. Hours became days and what started off as a fun way to pass time became a relentless addiction, eating away at my time and my brain.

Despite my hours of practice, I still faced the same screen of defeat over and over again. An unsettling sense of insanity crept in as I mindlessly repeated the same patterns over and over again, becoming Sisyphus trying to roll my ball of tetrominoes up the hill. Had I wasted my time? Was I chasing an unattainable goal?

Growing up in the information age meant having access to vast information and rapidly advancing tools at a young age. Screens weren't luxuries but rather familiar parts of my environment I relied on every day. With the exponential growth of computing power and the development that came with it, entertainment evolved into a digital form, offering immersive stories and "realer-than-real" graphics that offered easy access into different worlds at a click of a button.

But despite all that, I chose to play a far simpler game: Tetris.

I was introduced to the game of Tetris through a video I received by a friend. It was a showcase of the skills of a player named "Doremy". I didn't expect much, even anticipating a pitifully boring black-and-white game made of pixels. A few seconds passed, and suddenly in the blink of an eye, Doremy maneuvered blocks into a neat stack. It seemed like an inhuman feat; no human could move with such precision and speed. However, much to my surprise, I discovered a new YouTube rabbit hole full of a surprisingly advanced playing, strategy, and even theory about the game.

After a few mindless hours scrolling through videos of blindingly fast blocks, I realized that I could simply try it for myself. I logged onto TETR.IO (the most popular form of modern Tetris) and I quickly picked up the basics from what I had seen, but a quick introduction to the game became a consuming task of practicing every night. Hours blurred into days, days blurred into months, and I became fixated on the game. Top players became my idols, the Tetris wiki pages became my encyclopedia, and my keyboard became a tool just for the game. Despite the immense amount of time I had put in, it seemed like a Sisyphean effort. I was rolling a ball of tetrominoes up a hill without any end in sight. 

It seemed I would never carve out a place for my name among the best. I was contemplating if the persistence was even worth it; their years of experience outpaced mine, and I felt like I had been hitting my head on the same wall over and over again.

Just then, I had an idea. I could use my background in coding, something other players didn't have, and leverage it to improve faster than any before me. I had nothing to lose, so I decided to go all in. I would create a simulation of the game, then improve it over time so that I could learn from it myself in the places where human error had failed me.

The bot I had created started off slow and inaccurate, but only after a few days, I was able to use my coding skills to make a convincingly solid algorithm. After watching through countless tournament matches and coding in moves, I started to think about the game in computer logic rather than game logic. I learned advanced moves, starting with the very basic "T-spin" into advanced parity and graph theory, and eventually was able to subconsciously pull off intricate moves in a split second. Tetris became my second language, with its own nuances and secrets that were only noticeable at a high level of play.

In my own unique way, I had made my way into the highest ranks of Tetris, now being able to face off against the very same players I once watched in admiration. The long pursuit of greatness was finally over.
