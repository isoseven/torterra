## operators
- **the conjunction operator**
	- denoted as $p \land q$ (AND)
		- example:
		  $p$ = "i will have salad for lunch"
		  $q$ = "i will have salmon for dinner"
		  $p \land q$ = "i will have salad for lunch **AND** i will have salmon for dinner"
	- truth table: 
	  ![truth-table-and.png|163x159](/img/user/zimages/1-14-2026/truth-table-and.png)

- **the disjunction operator**
	- denoted as $p \lor q$ (OR)
	- also called inclusive or
		- example:
		  $p$ = "that car has a bad engine"
		  $q$ = "that car has a bad carburetor"
		  $p \lor q$ = "that car either has a bad engine **OR** a bad carburetor"
	- truth table:
	  ![truth-table-or.png|168x154](/img/user/zimages/1-14-2026/truth-table-or.png)
- **exclusive or operator**
	- denoted as $p \oplus q$  (XOR)
		- example:
		  $p$ = "i will earn an A in this course"
		  $q$ = "i will drop this course"
		  $p \oplus q$ = "i will either earn an A in this course or i will drop it (but not both)"
	- $\lnot$ and $\oplus$ together are **not** universal.
		- universal: can create any other logic gate (not/and, not/or)
	- truth table:
	  ![truth-table-xor.png|165x157](/img/user/zimages/1-14-2026/truth-table-xor.png)
- **implication operator
	- denoted as $p \rightarrow q$
	- it is false **only** in the case that p is **TRUE** but q is **FALSE**.
	- **remember:** if p is **FALSE** then it will HAVE to be **TRUE**.
		- example:
		  $p$ = "i am elected"
		  $q$ = "i will lower taxes"
		  $p \rightarrow q$ = "if i am elected, then i will lower taxes"
	- **some terminology**:
		- inverse = $\lnot p \rightarrow \lnot q$
		- converse: $q \rightarrow p$
			- logically equivalent to inverse
		- contrapositive: $\lnot q \rightarrow \lnot p$
			- contrapositive is the same as implication (doing it twice reverts it)
	- truth table:
	  ![truth-table-implication.png|165x149](/img/user/zimages/1-14-2026/truth-table-implication.png)
- **biconditional operator**
	- denoted as $p \leftrightarrow q$
	- it is true when both $p \rightarrow q$ and $q \rightarrow p$ are true.
	- this is also the same as $\lnot(p \oplus q)$
		- example:
		  $p$ = "it will rain today"
		  $q$ = "the football team won the game"
		  $p \leftrightarrow q$ = "it will rain today if and only if the football team wins the game"
	- truth table:
	  ![truth-table-biconditional.png|148x136](/img/user/zimages/1-14-2026/truth-table-biconditional.png)

> [!warning] 
> English by itself is ambiguous regarding the "or"
> In this class "OR" will always mean the **disjunction operator**.

## bits and bit operators
- **bits** are binary digits. 1/0
- **bit string** is an ordered series/sequence of 1 or more bits.
	- bit strings are always written **left to right**.
	- by convention, the **first bit on the left** is the most significant bit.
		- 1101 = 8+4+1 = 13
- **bitwise operators**
	- bitwise or, and, xor, etc is similar to a truth table. they are binary operators so they require two bit strings.
